Vision

In our world, more web pages, digital tools, algorithms, and data points are born than people. We need to learn to live together with this digital species (the ‘technology’ collective). We need to learn to collaborate, but in this, not forget our human strengths: we should let intuition and data collaborate. Going from probabilities to possibilities, from logic to wisdom, and let our algorithms and emotions collaborate.

I believe living in such a world is possible. However, it does require the necessary changes, of which most within our attitude and mind-set. Through our designer skills, creating experiences and artifacts, we invite our public into our ideas and visions. I want to allow ‘normal people’ to fantasize and dream, to dive deep into an engaging story and let their mind wander freely within that world. I believe this can be done through speculative design and design fiction. By closing the gap between reality and fiction, I want to allow people to dream and ‘think big’ again, to think of life as if we could change it. Just like I believe we actually can.

I believe to design is to be a pragmatist. Derived from the work of William James [X], some important theories of pragmatism are radical empiricism (the relation between things and the things themselves form an experience), pluralism (there is no one truth), anti-absolutism (there is no truth already ‘out there’ for us to discover), and habits of action (beliefs are not static but are put into action). In design work, this results in 3 core principles:

There should be equal emphasis on the things that actually are, as how we experience them. We should understand the temporality of life and embrace constant change; leave room for complexity and ambiguity, and keep questioning the things that we already think we understand.

Stay pragmatic, stay in the here and now; see actions and habits as mechanisms of the metaphysical, and act using the same language. Produce practical tools that play to our bodily experiences.

So, to talk about alternative positions of the future, we should tinker and experiment with what’s around us. Make it physical and experiential.

As a designer, I aim to leave room for wonder, magic, and spirituality in this world full of technology. I believe design is much more than ‘making’; it is ‘making things possible’ [X].

Professional Identity

I am a sensitive data-designer that combines intuition and philosophy with data, technology & code, to speculate about alternative perspectives on the future everyday.

I grew up in a household of Ying and Yang. Where my mom would relief my stomach aches by ‘pulling it out’ with her hands and taught me that energies can be steered to manifest your desires, my dad would help me pick apart objects to figure out their internal workings and taught me that everything in life can be explained and everything lives in matter.

This has led to a duality that I have felt throughout my whole life. Where on the one side I am practical, analytical, and scientific, I on the other side love to get lost in mythical stories, highly value art and social practices, and partake in philosophical discussion. Being raised by parents that clearly embody both one of these characters, I have always felt that I had to choose between these two forms of being. But today I am starting to learn that we can combine the two, let them co-exist.

As a designer, I aspire to let intuition and rationale collaborate. Where can sensitivity and intuition be part of data analysis? How can code and data speak to emotions and become spiritual? I am now learning to see where they can be combined and empower each other, where the aesthetics of intelligence can be found [X].

In this, I use methodologies that strongly rely on Creativity and Aesthetics, and work with data and computing tools deriving from the Math, Data and Computing field. In my work as a designer, I aim to implement both powers through sensitivity and practicality; creating things with hardware, software, philosophy, and speculations. I aim to leave room for ambiguity and complexity, while providing concrete (technical) tools and products.

In this section, I look back at my motivations for choosing to pursue the masters Industrial Design at the TU/e, and describe the intentionalities of my personal development from during my studies.

Past

Before my masters, I completed the bachelors of Industrial Design at the TU/e as well. During my bachelors, I did an internship at Design Agency MMID in Delft [X]. In this internship, I was looking explicitly to develop my hard skills and wanted to learn more about the ‘finalizing’ and end stages of designing, something that I really missed in my education at the time. The internship could very well provide me in this, however I learned that while very interesting, the skills and processes closer to releasing a product were not the ones I felt like I should develop further in. I learned to value the skills I developed at Eindhoven, which when talking about product development stage belong more to the front-end, even more than I had before. Specifically, I am talking about critical thinking, unraveling conceptual and contextual complexity, approaching design from a visionary and transforming standpoint. Those were the attributes I had as a designer that made me stand out at MMID, and that made me capable of adding something valuable to the projects there. After my bachelors I continued to work at MMID, first part-time during my FBP and after graduating full-time. In this experience, I liked the projects most where I could contribute conceptually and visionary, working on the bigger picture of the project. This is in the end why I ended up choosing to pursue my masters at the TU/e as well; I felt that those things I valued most about my work were very ‘TU/e-style’, and I saw the infancy of it, feeling that I could still learn so much on this topic in the masters ID. Next to this, my experience of working in the industry reinforced my appreciation for the opportunities the academic world provides. It allowed me to still focus on my personal development, work on projects without financial or market interests, and left room for both deep and broad experimentation and explorations. This mentality is also seen back in my development focus within my masters. Throughout my courses and projects, I worked a lot within the speculative design [X] and design friction [X] realm, something that mostly lives within the academic world. I think within these design approaches, exactly the skills and attitudes that I valued as designer come forward strongly, enabling me to develop myself within. Next to this learning focus within Creativity and Aesthetics, I also wanted to develop myself within the Math, Data and Computing expertise area. While mostly driven by my enjoyment in working with code and data, and interests in new technologies, there was also an industry aspect here. Skills within this area will only grow in the upcoming years, giving me a stronger position on the market after graduating. So, in my masters I set out to investigate where and how an integration between these two aspects can exist; critical and visionary design, and working with data and computing techniques. The course ‘Creativity and aesthetics of data and AI’ couldn’t be more perfect. Seeing the application of futuring and working with MDC-focused tools like LLMs and ML, was eye opening. Throughout my masters, I kept referring my work to the aesthetics of intelligence introduced in the course [X]. When coming closer to graduating, new development questions arose; I know I like doing this speculative-design-friction work, but how does that receive a space in the industry?

Projects

Final Master Project - Spiritual Technology: Designing Space for Spirituality in the Everyday

Data Enabled Design

This course introduces the Data Enabled Design methodology [X], and with my group we applied this to study the behavior of people in their living space with a focus on mental health. Through first researching the context of behavior in a specific setting with sensor probes and data collection, we then, in the informed step, intervene the setting with an adjustable light design.

This course demonstrates my skills within prototyping and the integration of software and hardware that enables remote data collection. Furthermore it demonstrates both qualitative and quantitative data analysis, and how they are combined within the inquiry, to then show how these data insights drive the design choices in the project.

The development of these skills allowed me to integrate the collection and analysis of (quantitative) data, for example in my FMP. It established a familiarity with integrating DataFoundry and using OOCSI, making me more easily apply these tools in other projects. This allowed me to come closer to my desired identity as designer; becoming a data designer that integrates data science in her process.

In this section, I reflect on my work approach, the application of the expertise areas within, and my professional skills. For this I mostly refer to my Final Master Project.

Present

Over the years of being a designer, I have learned ‘how I work’. This means I know what I need to proceed within the process and where my strengths and weaknesses lie. I’ve learnt that I need cooperation in my projects; my creativity flows best if I can speak out loud, spar with others and continue with new ideas, inspiration and input. In order to achieve this, I organize and plan for co-creations, collaborations in projects, meetings with experts, and surround myself in collaborative environments. A clear example of this is my collaboration with C°F. I specifically looked for expertise within designing with data; enhancing my MDC skills that allow me to collect, analyse, and visualize with digital data. I continued with this collaboration in my FMP because of the fit I feel with the studio and to keep collaboration flowing through co-creation and reflection sessions with colleagues, and presenting my work in design meetups. When collaborating with others, clear communication is key. Giving clear project status update presentations to my colleagues allowed me to receive concrete and detailed feedback. Over the semesters, I have gotten better and better at this, even receiving an honorable mention in a pitch competition during the prototype-day at C°F, stating that “the only intern at C°F gave the best presentation”.

Integration of Areas of Expertise

First of all, my work fully lives within the integration of MDC and CA. During my masters, my work was focused on questioning the role of technology in our everyday life, and how we can work towards a future in which spiritual aesthetics can be found in technology. Think about feeling data visualizations or speculative design on digital twins. Next to this, I make use of AI tools in the creative process, to inspire, collaborate, or bring in new perspectives, working with the Aesthetics of Intelligence [X]. Secondly, inspired by the work of Lee, I use “technology as our moral mirror” [X]. In my work I show notions of post phenomenology and pragmatism in design. Both these integrations require expertise in both TR and US; building prototypes and understanding technological functioning (TR) and seeing societal positioning, understanding social structures and user behavior (US). The attitude of pragmatism in design does something very similar: using making skills to philosophize, working towards an understanding for human and societal complexities. Lastly, throughout my design process, I integrate all the areas of expertise, each of them having their own purpose in the process. Future

After obtaining my Masters degree of Industrial Design, I have plans for both my professional and personal journey. Professionally, I see myself working within a small design studio, becoming a true team member and content-designer. While I do have an eye for what is needed within a design process, how the next steps can be executed, and for making informed decisions, I do not see myself as a project manager (yet). This is because I have a big love for diving deep, staying close to the content and being able to make and create with my hands (or digital tools). While within my field of interest a PhD would be a more logical career choice, due to its academic freedom and lack of commercial drive, I think a (small) design studio is a better fit for me. Compared to a PhD, it offers more variety of work, more collaboration, and more freedom in design approach. However, for me important is that there is still space for my critical- philosophical attitude, and room for questioning and research. I am therefore going to look for a visionary company, working on installations and pioneering projects, that has a culture that encourages growth, exploration and collaboration. Examples of this are studios like Falkland Studio, Fiction Factory, and Designwolf. In the far future, I might even start a small design (research) studio myself. I think this offers the opportunity to have full ownership in company vision, while staying close to content as well. However, before I feel comfortable doing this, I want to have developed enough specialized skills, a big enough network, and partnerships that want to take this challenge with me. Having learned how important traveling and following ambitions also are for my personal (& professional!) development, I want to travel before fully committing to a professional career. I want to explore southern and eastern Europe by bike: connecting with nature and my surroundings, using only my own strength and a very small collection of possessions to get around. For me the challenge here lies mostly in learning to live small, and needing to connect with the environment and locals to find my way, meet new people, and have a place to stay.
